Culture and History

Discover the rich tapestry of Brazilian culture and history. From the vibrant streets of São Paulo to the historic streets of Salvador, Brazil is a land of diverse traditions, art, and heritage. Explore the unique blend of African, Indigenous, and Portuguese influences that shape the country's unique identity.

Culture in São Paulo

São Paulo, Brazil’s largest and most cosmopolitan city, is home to a vibrant and growing Afro-Brazilian cultural movement. With millions of Afro-descendants living in the city, São Paulo has become a powerful stage for Black art, literature, music, and activism. Neighborhoods like Liberdade and Zona Sul are known for their strong community ties and cultural expressions. Afro-Brazilian influence is felt through samba schools, hip-hop movements, and Afro-house parties, as well as in spaces like the Museu Afro Brasil in Ibirapuera Park, which celebrates African heritage and Black resistance in Brazil. The city also hosts events like Marcha do Orgulho Crespo (Pride in Natural Hair March), uplifting Black identity and beauty.

Culture in Belém

Belém, in the northern Amazon region, has a different but equally rich Afro-Brazilian legacy. African traditions blended with Indigenous and Portuguese influences, creating unique cultural expressions. The Carimbó dance and music style, often accompanied by drums and flutes, has Afro roots and is a source of pride in the region. Religious traditions such as Tambor de Mina and Terecô—Afro-Indigenous spiritual practices—are part of the sacred cultural life in Belém. The city’s markets, such as Ver-o-Peso, showcase the fusion of African culinary and herbal traditions, while festivals like Círio de Nazaré also reflect layered identities shaped by African presence.

Culture in Recife

Recife, Pernambuco’s vibrant capital, is a cultural mosaic shaped by centuries of diverse influences. Its colonial architecture, especially in nearby Olinda, tells the story of Portuguese rule, while its cobblestone streets echo with the rhythms of frevo and maracatu—traditional music and dance that electrify the city, especially during Brazil’s most artistic Carnival. Recife is also a hub of literary, theatrical, and folk traditions, with venues like the Casa da Cultura showcasing crafts and performances from across the state. The city’s deep Afro-Brazilian roots add to its unique flavor, with spiritual practices, cuisine, and celebrations reflecting a blend of Indigenous, African, and European heritage. Whether it's the beat of a drumline or the scent of regional dishes like feijoada and bolo de rolo, Recife is alive with culture at every corner.
