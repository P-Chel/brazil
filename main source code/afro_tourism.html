Afro-tourism

Discover the powerful legacy and living spirit of Afro-Brazilian heritage. In the streets of Salvador, the drums of Candomblé echo stories of strength and identity. Through music, dance, food, and faith, Afro-tourism in Brazil connects you to a rich, resilient history that continues to inspire.

Afro-tourism in Salvador

Salvador, Bahia is often considered the heart of Afro-Brazilian culture. As the first colonial capital of Brazil and a major port in the Atlantic slave trade, Salvador became a melting pot of African traditions that are still deeply rooted in the city's identity today. From the vibrant beats of samba and axé music, to capoeira performances in the streets, and the colorful religious traditions of Candomblé, Afro culture is celebrated in daily life. The Pelourinho district, with its colonial architecture and cultural centers, serves as a hub for Afro-Brazilian music, dance, and art. Traditional Bahian cuisine, like acarajé and moqueca, also reflects strong African influences.

Afro-tourism in Recife

Recife, Pernambuco, while lesser known than Salvador, has its own rich Afro-Brazilian heritage. The city’s carnival features frevo and maracatu, Afro-influenced rhythms and dances that energize the streets with music and movement. Recife’s connection to African heritage is also seen in its religious practices, with Candomblé and other syncretic religions playing a significant role in the spiritual life of the community. The Casa da Cultura and the neighborhoods of Olinda and Santo Amaro preserve the memory of Afro-Brazilian contributions to Pernambuco’s history, resistance, and identity. Together, Salvador and Recife offer a powerful immersion into Afro-Brazilian culture—alive in the music, food, spirituality, and resilient spirit of their people.
